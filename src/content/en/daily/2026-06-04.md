## 3. Trump Signs Revised AI Executive Order: Establishes Voluntary Model Review System

Less than two weeks after scrapping the original executive order, President Trump signed a revised version of the AI executive order. The new version, adopted after senior advisors persuaded him to return to the issue, establishes a voluntary model review system — tech companies can voluntarily submit models for government assessment while promising to promote innovation and security. The policy represents a turning point in the White House's AI governance approach, though it is expected to face criticism from both regulation supporters and deregulation advocates.

> **Awesome AI View:** The shift from mandatory review to voluntary submission reflects the core tension in US AI policymaking: how to balance safety with innovation competitiveness. While voluntary review creates a framework for government participation in model assessment, the "voluntary" qualifier may significantly limit practical regulatory impact. The long-term challenge remains finding the right balance between innovation speed and public safety.

## 4. xAI Sued by UK MP: Grok Deepfake Images Trigger Landmark Liability Test

UK Labour MP Jess Asato has filed a lawsuit against xAI over sexually explicit deepfake images of her generated by the Grok model. According to the Financial Times, this is the first high-profile test of whether AI companies can be held legally responsible for their generated content. xAI has also asked the court to strip the anonymity protection from the plaintiffs, forcing them to choose between revealing their real identities or dropping the lawsuit. xAI is simultaneously facing investigations in the EU, UK, and California.

> **Awesome AI View:** This case's legal significance extends far beyond the individual lawsuit — it will set precedent for liability attribution of AI-generated content. If AI companies are found liable for their model outputs, it will fundamentally reshape the industry's risk models and operational frameworks. xAI's strategy of demanding plaintiff de-anonymization also raises broader questions about victim protection and judicial fairness.
